INTERVIEW

'Don't do Singham, learn to do pandemic policing'

NEWSONE.ORG CORRESPONDENT
Bhopal, May 28, 2021

 

 

Amid second wave of Coronavirus that has taken a heavy toll of human lives and seen stricter lockdown across the country but at the same time several incidents of misbehaviour too with people on streets by police personnel and administrative authorities there is sane advice to the law enforcement agencies from Former Director General rank Indian Police Service (IPS) officer from Madhya Pradesh Dr Shailendra Shrivastava- Pls don't do Singham, learn to do pandemic policing.

In an interview with NewsOne.org the 1986 batch Indian Police Service (IPS) officer officer feels this is high time the governments and top brass of the police department take initiative to train our police force and even executive magistrates on acquiring soft skills.

Here are excerpts:

 

NewsOne.org: There have been incidents coming to light at regular intervals during pandemic time regarding policemen misbehaving with people on streets during lockdown. How do you see such incidents?

Dr Shailendra Shrivastava: This is wrong and unacceptable. This shouldn't have happened.There is difference between Army and police. Army is supposed to shoot to kill the enemy whereas police are supposed to use minimum force to immobilize the enemy hiding in public.

I joined the IFS having completed my studies in Benaras Hindu University (BHU). By the time I joined Indian Police Service (IPS) and was in National Police Academy for training I had as many as 10 papers published in international journals.I thought I would get appreciation in the Academy over my academic achievements. But one day the then director of the academy called me to his office. Much to my astonishment he told me that I had joined a wrong force. When I asked why he said to me, "You should not expect bouquets but brickbats in police force and you should always be prepared to listen to criticism." 

 

His words were so genuine and powerful that it changed my outlook about policing forever. 

We have come a long way from colonial police era. We are now in the age of community policing in which we are supposed to look for enemies among people in society. We can't behave like a colonial era police.

 

Photo courtesy: news18.com

NewsOne.org: Is there any training required for the police force to deal with people in such a situation?

 

Dr Shailendra Shrivastava : Certainly. We have realised that for the past 100 years we hardly thought of such a pandemic affecting the entire globe. Police have been engaged in disaster management from time to time but such a pandemic like Covid-19 was hardly imagined before by none of us. We were not prepared to face such a kind of catastrophic situation. The policing today includes community policing as well. However, we didn't work on community policing as much as we should have done. Otherwise, these unfortunate incidents would have hardly taken place on such a scale.

It's not that police force didn't do anything. The fact remains police force did its job in an excellent manner but a few incidents of aberrations created much media hype. Such incidents cast a shadow over good work done by the police force.

There are several police authorities and other men in uniform in the department who embarked upon novel ideas to motivate the police force and persuade people to comply with Covid-19 protocols. For instance, we saw the then inspector general (IG) of police in Indore Vivek Sharma sing to create awareness in public on Covid-19 protocol and motivate the police force. There have been several other such instances too but unfortunately any policeman hitting a pandemic law violator with a baton or a district magistrate slapping a man and damaging his mobile phone become headlines in the media the next day but hardly anyone remembers the good work as was done by Vivek Sharma and others.

 

NewsOne.org: Does a policeman or a district magistrate have power to slap or beat any person who has violated the lockdown rules?

Dr Shailendra Shrivastava: No, they don't have any authority to do that. There are certain situations when police can resort to use of minimum force but not in such a situation when anyone is found violating the lockdown or not complying with Covid-19 protocol. A violator can be booked under relevant sections of certain Act but abusing, slapping or beating him is unacceptable. Even high court issued directive in certain cases that prohibits police force or administrative authorities from indulging in such misbehaviour but, unfortunately, we continue to witness such incidents at regular intervals from various parts of the country.

 

NewsOne.org: Are such incidents of misbehaviour a fallout of police personnel working long hours and under tremendous pressure?

Dr Shailendra Shrivastava: This is true that a policeman has to work for 16 to 18 hours at a stretch day in and day out during this pandemic time and he or she is under tremendous pressure to perform to protect people from Covid-19 but it doesn't give them a licence to hit or abuse anyone. Those in police force know well that they have to work under adverse circumstances. If they can't handle such stress and pressure they should leave the police force. Why should they be there?

 

NewsOne.org: Don't you think our policing needs to be changed irrespective of the situation- pandemic or no pandemic?

Dr Shailendra Shrivastava: There is a joke doing the rounds for a long time in police force and outside. Visiting India a Russian went to a remote police station. He wanted to see the lie detector machine, finger print unit etc in the police station and wanted to know how police force collected the evidence in any crime. The police station in charge asked the Russian to wait and said they had a multi-purpose equipment which worked for them for everything. Then an accused of theft was brought before the Russian and a policeman hit him with a baton. The accused immediately confessed to having committed the crime. The police station in charge then showed the baton to the Russian and said this was their multi-purpose equipment which worked as a lie detector machine and produced all the evidence of crime they needed to corroborate the charges.

Jokes apart, policing requires changes but at the moment we are talking of the pandemic situation, hence my emphasis is on pandemic policing. My advice to the governments and police force in all the states is to train the police personnel on soft skills.

 

NewsOne.org: How, in your view, this training should be done?

Dr Shailendra Shrivastava: We should engage psychologists, sociologists, health workers, social workers and senior police officers besides others to impart institutionalised training not only to police personnel but also executive magistrates. We don't need Singhams on the streets but police personnel trained in soft skills in such a situation. At the same time, there should be anger management training as well for them. This should be done without any delay as it's already too late for us.

 

NewsOne.org: Don't you think the governments including its law enforcing agencies have failed us during the pandemic which has claimed a heavy toll of human lives.

Dr Shailendra Shrivastava: Why single out any institution or individual? I would like to say that we all have failed in our duty as a society in protecting ourselves from Coronavirus and checking its spread. We need to follow the Covid-19 protocol religiously in the first place.
